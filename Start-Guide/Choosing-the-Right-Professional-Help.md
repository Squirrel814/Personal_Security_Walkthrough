# Choosing the Right Professional Help

**Personal Security Investigation Framework**  
Version 1.0 | Cross-Platform

This companion to [When & How to Escalate](When-and-How_to-Escalate.md) helps you decide **who** to contact after you have documented your findings. It is not legal advice — when laws or jurisdiction matter, ask a qualified attorney.

---

## Start here

You have already done the hard part: **documenting calmly**. Use your investigation log, file inventory, and captures as the foundation for every conversation below.

If you have not prepared your evidence package yet, complete [How to Prepare Before Escalating](When-and-How_to-Escalate.md#how-to-prepare-before-escalating) first.

---

## Quick decision guide

| Your situation (examples) | Who to contact first | Why |
|---------------------------|----------------------|-----|
| Unauthorized charges, account takeover, crypto theft | **Bank / financial institution** + consider **incident response (IR)** | Stop ongoing financial harm; preserve transaction records |
| Ransomware, locked files, ransom note | **IR firm** or trusted IT pro; **law enforcement** if you choose to report | Recovery and evidence preservation are time-sensitive |
| Confirmed malware, persistence, data theft | **Incident response / digital forensics** | Technical containment and professional imaging |
| Stalking, harassment, files named after you, doxxing | **Local law enforcement** (non-emergency or cyber unit) + document everything | Personal targeting may be criminal; LE guides preservation |
| Unsure but multiple red flags | **Trusted IT consultant** or **IR** for triage | Low-friction second opinion before deeper paths |
| Mostly clean scans, anxiety only | **No rush** — continue light monitoring; optional trusted IT chat | Escalation is for evidence, not fear alone |

**Emergency:** If you are in immediate physical danger, call your local emergency number first — not this guide.

---

## Option 1: Incident response / digital forensics firms

**Best for:** Confirmed or strongly suspected compromise, financial loss, sophisticated malware, business devices, or when you need professional imaging and containment.

**What they typically do:**
- Triage remotely or on-site
- Preserve evidence (forensic images, logs)
- Contain active threats
- Produce a technical report for you (and sometimes for insurers or counsel)

**How to find one:**
- Your employer's IT or security team (work devices)
- Cyber insurance carrier recommended vendor list
- Regional IR firms with digital forensics practice
- Referrals from a trusted attorney or accountant (not random cold calls)

**Questions to ask before hiring:**
1. Do you handle **consumer / home user** cases or only enterprise?
2. What is your **initial consultation** process and cost range?
3. Will you work from my **existing documentation** (log, inventory, captures)?
4. Do you perform **forensic imaging** without wiping my drive first?
5. What should I **stop doing** on the device until you advise otherwise?

---

## Option 2: Law enforcement

**Best for:** Personal targeting, harassment, stalking via digital means, fraud reports, or when you want an official record. Not a substitute for IR when you need immediate technical recovery.

**United States (examples):**
- **Local police** — non-emergency line; ask for cybercrime or fraud unit if available
- **[FBI IC3](https://www.ic3.gov)** — Internet Crime Complaint Center (online report; useful for fraud and cybercrime documentation)
- **FTC** — identity theft and consumer fraud resources at [reportfraud.ftc.gov](https://reportfraud.ftc.gov)

**Other countries:** Search for your national cybercrime reporting portal or police cyber unit.

**What to bring:**
- Your evidence package (see [project structure template](shared-templates/templates/project_structure_recommendation.md))
- Timeline in plain language
- Financial records if money is involved (redact unrelated account details)

**Expect:** Cases vary widely by jurisdiction and severity. A report creates a record even if immediate action is limited.

---

## Option 3: Trusted IT professional or consultant

**Best for:** Triage when you are not ready for full IR, second opinion on scan results, or help hardening after a clean investigation.

**Look for:**
- Someone you already trust (friend's recommendation, local shop with clear ethics)
- Willingness to **read your documentation** before remote access
- No pressure to "wipe and reinstall" before evidence is preserved

**Caution:** Not all IT generalists are incident responders. If they find serious persistence or theft, escalate to IR or LE.

---

## Option 4: Banks and financial institutions

**Best for:** Unauthorized transactions, account access, card fraud, or identity theft with financial impact.

**Do immediately:**
- Call the number on the back of your card or your bank's fraud line
- Document call times and reference numbers in your investigation log
- Ask how to **freeze or replace** affected accounts

This can run **in parallel** with IR or law enforcement — it is not either/or when money is involved.

---

## What this guide does not cover

- **DIY forensic imaging** — see [How to Create a Forensic Image](How-to-Create-a-Forensic-Image.md); default is **hand off to professionals** for full disk images when stakes are high
- **Legal strategy** — consult a lawyer for custody, liability, or court proceedings
- **Insurance claims** — your policy may require specific IR vendors or timelines

---

## After you choose

1. Log **who you contacted**, **when**, and **what you sent** in your investigation log.
2. Do not delete evidence unless a professional instructs you.
3. Return to [When & How to Escalate — After You Escalate](When-and-How_to-Escalate.md#after-you-escalate) for ongoing steps.
